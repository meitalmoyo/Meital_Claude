# Mediation — ch01: Conflict Theory

Foundational frameworks for understanding conflict. Bar Ilan Master's context.

---

## What Is Conflict?

A conflict exists when two or more parties perceive their goals, needs, or values as incompatible.

Key word: **perceive**. The conflict can be real or based on a misunderstanding. The mediator's job often involves clarifying whether the incompatibility is real or perceived.

---

## Types of Conflict

**By content:**
- **Substantive conflict** — disagreement over resources, money, property, facts
- **Relational conflict** — about the relationship itself: trust, respect, power, history
- **Value conflict** — different beliefs, priorities, worldviews (hardest to resolve)
- **Process conflict** — disagreement about how decisions are made, not what the decision is

**By intensity (escalation scale — Glasl):**
1. Hardening — positions become rigid
2. Debate and polemics — parties argue to convince
3. Actions, not words — behavior replaces dialogue
4. Images and coalitions — dehumanization begins, allies recruited
5. Loss of face — public humiliation, no going back
6. Threat strategies — ultimatums
7. Limited destruction — willing to accept some loss to harm the other
8. Fragmentation of the enemy — destroy the other's will to act
9. Together into the abyss — self-destruction acceptable if the other is destroyed

**Mediators are most effective at levels 1–4.** At levels 5+, structural intervention may be needed first.

---

## Root Causes of Conflict

**Moore's Circle of Conflict:**
- Relationship — poor communication, strong emotions, past events
- Data — lack of information, different information, different interpretations
- Interest — competing needs (tangible, procedural, psychological)
- Structural — external forces: time pressure, resource scarcity, physical setup
- Value — different beliefs about what is good, right, or important

**Why this matters for mediation:** Diagnose the root cause before proposing solutions. A data conflict needs information, not compromise. A value conflict may not be resolvable at all — but the relationship can still be managed.

---

## Conflict as Opportunity

Deutsch's principle: conflict is not inherently destructive. How parties handle it determines whether it's constructive or destructive.

**Constructive conflict:**
- Leads to change, growth, understanding
- Parties maintain respect
- Focus on the problem, not the person

**Destructive conflict:**
- Escalates beyond the original issue
- Parties focus on winning, not solving
- Leaves lasting damage to the relationship

The mediator's role is to shift destructive conflict toward constructive engagement — not to eliminate the conflict.
