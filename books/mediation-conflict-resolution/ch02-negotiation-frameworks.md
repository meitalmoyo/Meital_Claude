# Mediation — ch02: Negotiation Frameworks

Core frameworks for interest-based negotiation and conflict resolution.

---

## The Harvard Method (Fisher & Ury — "Getting to Yes")

The most influential negotiation framework. Four principles:

1. **Separate the people from the problem**
   — Negotiate about the issue, not against the person. Acknowledge emotions without letting them derail the substance.

2. **Focus on interests, not positions**
   — A position is what someone demands. An interest is why they want it.
   - Position: "I want full custody."
   - Interest: "I need to feel like a real parent in my child's life."
   The interests often have more room for creative solutions than the positions do.

3. **Invent options for mutual gain**
   — Brainstorm before deciding. Separate the creative phase from the evaluative phase. One option is a take-it-or-leave-it. Five options creates a negotiation.

4. **Insist on objective criteria**
   — Base decisions on external standards (market value, legal precedent, expert opinion, established policy). Takes the ego out of the decision.

## BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement

What happens if you don't reach a deal?

**Your BATNA** = your best option outside this negotiation.
**Their BATNA** = their best option if this negotiation fails.

**Why it matters:**
- If your BATNA is strong, you can walk away. You have leverage.
- If your BATNA is weak, you need this deal more.
- A mediator should help both parties understand their BATNAs realistically — people often overestimate their outside options.

**ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)** = the overlap between what each side is willing to accept. If the ZOPAs don't overlap, no deal is possible without changing someone's BATNA or their assessment of the situation.

## Positions vs. Interests

| | Positions | Interests |
|--|----------|---------|
| Definition | What they demand | Why they want it |
| Flexibility | Low — they've committed publicly | High — multiple options can satisfy an interest |
| Example | "I want an apology" | "I need to feel my dignity was restored" |

**The mediator's job:** Peel back the position to find the interest. Ask "What's important to you about that?" not "Why do you want that?"

## Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Modes (TKI)

Five ways people handle conflict, on two dimensions: **assertiveness** (concern for own needs) and **cooperativeness** (concern for other's needs):

| Mode | Assertive | Cooperative | When it works |
|------|----------|------------|---------------|
| **Competing** | High | Low | Emergencies, when you're right and stakes are high |
| **Accommodating** | Low | High | When the relationship matters more than the outcome |
| **Avoiding** | Low | Low | When the issue is trivial or timing is wrong |
| **Collaborating** | High | High | When both parties have important needs and time is available |
| **Compromising** | Medium | Medium | When a good-enough deal is better than no deal |

**No mode is right or wrong.** The mediator's role is to help parties shift toward collaborating when appropriate.

## Principled vs. Positional Negotiation

| | Positional | Principled (Harvard) |
|--|-----------|---------------------|
| Goal | Win | Solve the problem |
| Style | Adversarial | Collaborative |
| Focus | Position | Interests |
| Result | One wins, one loses (or both lose) | Potential mutual gain |
| Relationship | Often damaged | Usually preserved |

## Israeli Negotiation Culture (Context)

- Israeli communication style tends to be direct, blunt, and fast-paced (low power distance, low context)
- "No" often means "not yet" or "make me a better offer" — not necessarily a final rejection
- Personal relationships matter — trust is built before business is done
- Shouting or high emotion in negotiation is not necessarily a sign of breakdown — it can be normal expression
- Building rapport before diving into substance is culturally expected (coffee, small talk, family questions)
