# Mediation — ch03: Mediation Process

Stages, structure, and the mediator's role. Based on Israeli mediation practice and Bar Ilan curriculum.

---

## What Is Mediation?

A voluntary, confidential process in which a neutral third party (the mediator) helps disputing parties communicate and reach their own agreement.

Key principles:
- **Voluntary** — parties choose to participate
- **Confidential** — what is said in mediation stays in mediation
- **Self-determination** — the parties decide the outcome, not the mediator
- **Neutral** — the mediator has no stake in the outcome
- **Impartial** — the mediator treats both parties equally

**The mediator does NOT:** decide who is right, give legal advice, impose solutions, take sides, or represent either party.

---

## The Stages of Mediation

### 1. Pre-Mediation / Intake
- Initial contact with each party (often separately)
- Explain the process, rules, confidentiality
- Assess whether the case is suitable for mediation
- Identify parties, issues, and what each party hopes to achieve
- Set logistics: time, place, format

**Red flags for unsuitability:** active domestic violence, one party has no capacity to participate, extreme power imbalance that can't be managed, criminal matter beyond the scope

---

### 2. Opening / Introduction
- Mediator introduces themselves and explains the process
- Ground rules: one person speaks at a time, respect, no interruptions
- Confidentiality agreement
- Each party briefly introduces themselves and their view of the situation

**Mediator's tone here:** Calm, warm, authoritative. This sets the emotional temperature for the whole session.

---

### 3. Storytelling / Information Gathering
- Each party tells their story uninterrupted
- Mediator listens actively — reflects, summarizes, acknowledges emotions
- Goal: understand each party's perspective, identify underlying interests
- **Do not rush this stage.** Feeling heard is a prerequisite for being open to solutions.

**Key skills here:** Active listening, reframing, open questions, emotional acknowledgment

---

### 4. Identifying Issues and Interests
- Mediator synthesizes what both parties said
- Identifies shared interests and areas of agreement (they exist, even if small)
- Lists the issues to be addressed — frames them as shared problems, not opposing positions
- "It sounds like what's important to both of you is..."

---

### 5. Generating Options
- Brainstorming phase — all ideas are welcome, no evaluation yet
- Mediator facilitates, does not impose
- May use caucus (private meetings with each party) to explore sensitive options
- Goal: expand the solution space before narrowing it

---

### 6. Evaluating and Negotiating
- Parties evaluate options against their interests
- Mediator helps reality-test ("what happens if you don't reach an agreement?")
- Supports give-and-take without pressure
- Caucus can be useful here for parties who need private space to consider

---

### 7. Agreement
- If parties reach agreement, write it down clearly and specifically
- Both parties sign
- Include who does what, by when, and how compliance is verified
- Vague agreements fall apart — specificity prevents future conflict

---

### 8. Closure
- Acknowledge the parties' effort and courage in engaging the process
- Clarify what happens next
- Leave the door open: "If new issues arise, mediation is available"

---

## Caucus (Private Sessions)

A tool the mediator uses to meet with each party separately.

**Use caucus when:**
- A party is reluctant to speak openly in joint session
- There are strong emotions that need to be processed privately
- You suspect a party has a hidden interest they won't share in front of the other
- Progress has stalled and a private conversation might unblock it

**Rules:**
- Be transparent that you use caucus with both parties
- Don't share what was said in caucus without permission
- Spend roughly equal time with each party
- Return to joint session when ready

## Israeli Mediation Context

- Family mediation (גישור משפחתי) is the most common and developed field in Israel
- Commercial mediation is growing
- Court-annexed mediation exists but voluntary mediation is separate
- Mediators in Israel must be certified — certification requires training and supervised hours
- The Israeli Mediation Association (איגוד המגשרים) sets standards
