# Mediation — ch04: Communication Skills

The practical tools a mediator uses in every session.

---

## Active Listening

Not just hearing — demonstrating that you've heard and understood.

**Elements:**
- **Attend** — full attention, no interrupting, open body language
- **Reflect** — mirror back the content: "So what you're saying is..."
- **Acknowledge emotions** — name the feeling without judgment: "It sounds like this situation has been really frustrating for you"
- **Summarize** — periodically pull together what you've heard to check understanding
- **Clarify** — ask before assuming: "Can you help me understand what you mean by...?"

**What active listening is NOT:**
- Nodding while thinking about your next question
- Waiting for your turn to talk
- Agreeing with everything said
- Giving advice or solutions in response to a story

---

## Reframing

Taking a negative, positional, or attacking statement and restating it in neutral, interest-based, forward-looking language.

**The shift:**
- From blame → to need
- From past → to future
- From position → to interest
- From attacking the person → to describing the problem

**Examples:**

| What was said | Reframe |
|--------------|---------|
| "He always lies to me" | "It sounds like trust and honesty are very important to you in this relationship" |
| "I want full custody, I won't accept less" | "What I'm hearing is that being actively present in your children's lives is essential to you" |
| "She's impossible to work with" | "It sounds like you're finding it difficult to feel heard in your working relationship" |

**Rule:** The reframe must be accurate — if it misses the mark, the party will correct you, and that's fine. Better to try and miss than to not reflect at all.

---

## Open vs. Closed Questions

**Open questions** — invite elaboration, can't be answered with yes/no
- "What happened from your perspective?"
- "What's most important to you about this?"
- "How has this situation affected you?"
- "What would a good outcome look like for you?"

**Closed questions** — yes/no, or specific factual answer
- "Did you receive the letter?"
- "Is Tuesday available?"
- Use for clarifying facts, not for exploring perspectives

**Rule:** In the storytelling and exploration phases, use mostly open questions. Closed questions narrow the conversation too early.

---

## Normalizing and Validating

**Normalizing** — making the party feel their reaction is understandable
- "It makes sense that you feel that way given what happened"
- "Many people in this situation would feel the same"

**Validating** — affirming the person's experience without agreeing with their position
- "I can see this has been really hard for you"
- Not: "You're right that he was wrong"

**Why it matters:** People can't problem-solve when they feel unheard or judged. Normalizing and validating lower the emotional temperature enough for rational engagement to begin.

---

## Summarizing

Use throughout the session to:
- Check your own understanding
- Show the party they've been heard
- Transition between phases
- Identify areas of common ground

**Formula:** "Let me make sure I've understood correctly. [Summary of their position/story]. Is that right?"

After both parties have spoken:
"What I'm hearing from both of you is that [shared interest/common ground]. And the main things you need to work out together are [list of issues]. Does that capture it?"

---

## Managing Emotion

**When a party becomes very emotional:**
1. Stop. Let them feel the emotion without rushing to fix it.
2. Acknowledge: "I can see this is really painful."
3. Give them time. Silence is not failure — it's space.
4. When they're ready, gently guide back: "When you're ready, I'd like to..."
5. If needed, take a break or move to caucus.

**When parties escalate against each other:**
1. Interrupt firmly but respectfully: "Let me pause us here for a moment."
2. Redirect to the mediator: "I'd like to hear from each of you separately about what's coming up."
3. Return to ground rules if needed.
4. Don't let attacks go unanswered — but don't take sides. Reframe the attack into an interest.

---

## Language of Mediation

**Use:**
- "It sounds like..." / "What I'm hearing is..."
- "Help me understand..."
- "What's important to you about that?"
- "What would that look like?"
- "What would need to happen for that to work?"

**Avoid:**
- "You should..." (advice)
- "I think you're right that..." (taking sides)
- "That's understandable, but..." (the "but" erases the validation)
- "Let's move on" (dismissive, can feel like the emotion was cut off)
