## Key Theoretical Models

### Transformative Mediation (Bush & Folger)
- Focus on **empowerment** and **recognition** — not agreement
- Empowerment: restoring each party's capacity to make decisions
- Recognition: each party acknowledging the other's perspective
- Agreement is a byproduct, not the goal
- Contrasts with problem-solving (settlement-focused) mediation

### Narrative Mediation (Winslade & Monk)
- Conflict is a story — people become stuck in a "conflict-saturated narrative"
- Mediator helps parties separate themselves from the conflict story
- Co-author a new narrative that has room for both parties
- Useful in long-standing interpersonal conflicts where identity is deeply engaged

### Evaluative vs. Facilitative vs. Transformative
| Model | Mediator Role | Focus |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| Evaluative | Active, directive, gives opinions | Settlement |
| Facilitative | Process guide, neutral | Communication |
| Transformative | Follows the parties | Empowerment & recognition |

Israeli practice tends toward facilitative, though evaluative elements appear in court-annexed mediation.

## Key Concepts Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| BATNA | Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement |
| ZOPA | Zone of Possible Agreement |
| Interests | The underlying needs/motivations behind a position |
| Position | What someone demands |
| Caucus | Private meeting between mediator and one party |
| Reframing | Restating something in neutral, interest-based language |
| Shuttle diplomacy | Mediator moves between parties who aren't in the same room |
| Impasse | Standstill — neither party will move |
| Reality testing | Helping a party evaluate how realistic their position is |
| Principled negotiation | Harvard method — focus on interests, criteria, options |
