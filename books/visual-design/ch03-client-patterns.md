# Visual Design — ch03: Client Patterns

What works and what doesn't, based on real MOYO Productions client experience.
Update this after every client project.

---

## Briefing Clients

**Get this before starting anything:**
- Dimensions and format (digital? print? what size?)
- Color palette (existing brand colors or open to suggestion?)
- Target audience (who is this for?)
- Deadline and deliverable format
- Reference examples (what do they like / hate?)

**The "I'll know it when I see it" client** — push back gently. Ask them to show you 3 examples they like. Even bad examples tell you something. Never start without any direction.

**Budget conversation** — have it early. Scope creep is a design tax. Define: how many revision rounds are included? What counts as a new request vs a revision?

---

## Revision Management

**Round 1 feedback** — usually about big things: layout, color, direction. This is normal.

**Round 2 feedback** — should be refinements only. If they're still changing direction in round 2, the brief wasn't clear. Stop and re-brief.

**"Can you just change one more thing?"** — after the agreed rounds are done, this is a scope change. Friendly but firm: "Happy to do that — it'll be a small additional charge."

**Presenting work:**
- Always present in context (mockup on a wall, business card in hand, phone screen). Flat files on white backgrounds don't sell.
- Present the reasoning before showing the design: "I went with bold typography here because your audience is..." — this frames the work before they react.
- Give them 2 options max, never 5. Too many choices = paralysis = more revisions.

---

## Common Client Types (Israeli Market)

**The "make it busy" client** — wants to fill every corner with text and graphics. Solution: show them a clean version AND a busy version side by side. The clean one almost always wins when seen together.

**The "I have a logo from 2003" client** — their brand assets are low-res or in the wrong format. Ask for the highest-res version. If none exists, offer a logo cleanup as an add-on.

**The fast turnaround client** — happy to pay rush rates? Define what "fast" means to them. "ASAP" could mean tomorrow or next week.

**The committee client** — multiple stakeholders, contradictory feedback. Try to identify the decision-maker and route final approval through them only.

---

## Delivering Work

**Always send:**
- Final deliverable in the format they need (PDF, PNG, JPG, editable file)
- A brief note explaining what's in the package
- File naming that makes sense to them, not just to you

**Never send:**
- Working .ai or .aep files without agreeing on this upfront
- Low-res files for print
- RGB files for professional print

---

## After the Project

- Ask for a testimonial while the work is fresh
- Note in the project folder: what worked, what was difficult, would you work with them again?
- If they're a repeat client, add any preferences to their project overview file
